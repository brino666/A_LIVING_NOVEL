How A Living Novel Works

1. This isn't a chatbot.

Each time you continue, you get a real chapter -- roughly 1,500-1,800 words, tight and purposeful rather than padded out -- not a quick reply. It takes a bit to write; that's the trade for something that reads like a book instead of a text thread.

2. The world moves whether you show up or not.

Time keeps passing for the people around you between visits. Come back after a gap and things will have changed -- you'll find out the way you would coming home from a trip: gradually, through what people say and do, not a summary dumped on you at login.

3. The "What do you do?" box is always your character's action.

Whatever you type is read as something your character does or says -- never a note to the story. To change direction -- end a relationship, skip town, take time off -- write it as an action: "I stop returning his calls," "I drive to the next town." The story follows.

4. You can dial the intensity -- within real limits.

Settings has a +/- control to make a world feel gentler or more intense than its baseline. There's no ceiling on the dial itself, but there is a hard ceiling on what the writing engine will produce, no matter how far you push it:

  • Dark, violent, morally complex, or unsettling fiction -- available, and can get genuinely intense.
  • Sexually explicit content -- not available, at any setting.
  • Gratuitous gore or torture written purely for shock -- moderated even at maximum.
  • Anything sexualizing minors -- never, under any circumstance, full stop.
  • Real-world harmful instructions dressed up as fiction -- not available.

If a chapter goes somewhere you don't want, dial it back, or just have your character leave.

5. Chapters are capped and don't roll over.

3 chapters per rolling 24 hours on the base plan, 6 on the higher tier. Unused chapters don't carry over -- bank them and burn through several at once and you mostly get the bartender washing the same glass while the jukebox works through its playlist, one song at a time, nothing happening, nothing happening, a car passing outside. There's only so much story in a day; stockpiling chapters dilutes the thing that makes this a living novel instead of a book you binge. The cap refreshes 24 hours after your first chapter in that window, not at a fixed clock time like midnight.

6. Each world is its own subscription.

Running two stories at once means two subscriptions -- there's no bundled unlimited tier. Switch between the ones you have from My Library.

7. Three free chapters on your first world, then a 2-day trial once you subscribe.

Your first world starts with 3 free chapters -- no subscription needed, enough to see the world actually respond to what you do. The free preview is limited to one world per account, so a second world needs an existing one already subscribed first. Subscribing starts a 2-day free trial before your card is charged, and your subscription renews automatically at the end of each billing period until you cancel. Cancel anytime from your account settings -- see the Terms of Service for the full billing and cancellation terms.

Tips for building a world that lasts

  • Genre and tone work well as a comma-separated mix -- tone words, a comparison, and a one-line premise together, e.g. "dark, foggy, sultry, Stephen King meets Anne Rice, a farmer's quiet obsession with growing the perfect broccoli sprout." The more specific and varied the mix, the more the story has to work with.
  • Your title gets read as a promise, not just a label. The story tends to take it literally and rush to fulfill it -- name a world "Lost" and don't be surprised if your character is physically lost by day two, even if you meant it emotionally. If you want a title's meaning to stay figurative or unfold slowly, say so in the tone field ("the title is metaphorical -- she isn't missing, she's adrift") or pick something more oblique.
